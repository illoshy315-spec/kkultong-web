# Korea Survival Tips — Traveler Guide (2025)
> Q&A for kkultongkorea.com | Foreign traveler perspective | as of 2025

### 🚌 Getting Around

Q: How do I get a T-Money card and where can I use it?
A: Buy a T-Money card at any convenience store (GS25, CU, 7-Eleven), subway station vending machine, or Incheon Airport for ₩2,500–₩5,000 (card only, you load money separately). Tap it on buses, subways, taxis nationwide — even at convenience stores and some vending machines. Fares are ₩100 cheaper per ride vs. cash, so it pays for itself fast. Top up with cash at any convenience store or subway machine.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/contentsView.do?vcontsId=140663; inmykorea.com/t-money-card-korean-transportation-card/

Q: What's the difference between KTX and SRT?
A: Both are high-speed trains (~300 km/h). KTX departs from Seoul Station, Yongsan, and more; SRT departs from Suseo Station (southeast Seoul) with fewer stops. SRT is ~10% cheaper (e.g. Seoul–Busan KTX ₩59,800 vs SRT ~₩52,700 as of 2025) but has a smaller network. Book KTX on letskorail.com or korail.com (English available). SRT is a separate operator (SR) — book at etk.srail.kr (English available). For tourists, the KORAIL PASS (consecutive 3/5-day options) covers KTX but not SRT.
Source: letskorail.com; korail.com/global/eng/main; etk.srail.kr

Q: Why won't Kakao T (Kakao Taxi) accept my foreign credit card?
A: Kakao T's in-app payment requires a Korean card. Workaround: set payment to "Pay to Driver" (cash or card at the taxi). Alternatives: Uber works in Korea (uses local taxis), K.Ride has English interface and accepts foreign cards, and TADA is another option. All three accept foreign credit cards without a Korean phone number.
Source: wise.com/en-jp/blog/taxi-apps-in-south-korea; kride.kakaomobility.com

Q: What's the best way from Incheon Airport to Seoul?
A: Three options. Fastest: AREX Express (₩13,000 adult, 43 min to Seoul Station, non-stop). Most convenient: Airport Limousine Bus (₩17,000–₩18,000, ~60–90 min, drops near major hotels). Most flexible: Taxi (~₩60,000–₩100,000, 50–70 min). For your first trip, AREX Express is the sweet spot — fast, cheap, and connects to Seoul Subway at Seoul Station. Buy AREX tickets at the airport or online at airportrailroad.com or on travel apps like Klook/Trazy for small discounts.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr; theseoulguide.com/airport-railroad-arex-express-train/; seoulexplorer.com/incheon-airport-to-seoul-station-arex-guide/

Q: What do the bus colors mean in Seoul?
A: Blue (파랑): main trunk routes connecting districts. Green (초록): branch buses feeding into subway stations and blue routes. Red (빨강): express buses to/from suburbs (higher fare, fewer stops). Yellow (노랑): circular routes within one major district. Just tap your T-Money when boarding AND when getting off — transfers between buses and subway within 30 min are free (up to 4 transfers).
Source: english.seoul.go.kr/service/movement/public-transportation/; topis.seoul.go.kr

Q: Where should I rent a car vs. use public transport?
A: Seoul and Busan: public transport is faster, cheaper, and parking is a nightmare. Rent a car for Jeju Island (₩50,000–₩100,000/day), Sokcho/Gangneung coastal area, and Gyeongju to explore spread-out historical sites. You'll need an International Driving Permit (get it before Korea — AAA for Americans). Book on Lotte Rent-a-Car or JejuOneCar with English sites.
Source: lotterentacar.net/hp/eng/reservation/index.do; kayak.com (Jeju car rental prices); helenabradbury.com (IDP requirements)

### 🏨 Accommodation

Q: Motel vs. guesthouse vs. hotel — what's the difference and what should I expect to pay?
A: Motels (모텔): ₩40,000–₩80,000/night, private room+bathroom, often nicer than expected with big TVs and PCs — perfectly fine for travelers. Guesthouses: ₩15,000–₩35,000/night for a dorm bed, social vibe, breakfast often included. Hotels: ₩80,000+ for Western chains. "Daesil" (대실) is day-use — you rent the room for 3–4 hours (₩20,000–₩40,000) for resting between check-out and a late flight. Totally normal, nothing sketchy.
Source: creatrip.com/en/blog/3174 (Korean motel culture); koreajoongangdaily.com (daesil); lonelyplanet.com (jjimjilbang guide)

Q: Can foreigners use Yanolja (야놀자) and Yeogi Eottae (여기어때)?
A: These Korean apps have the best motel prices but require a Korean phone number + ARC to book. Without one, use Booking.com or Agoda — they list the same motels at a small markup (₩5,000–₩15,000 more). For last-minute deals, walk into a motel — the prices on the sign outside ("현재요금") are walk-in rates and often cheaper than apps.
Source: tripadvisor.com.au (Seoul Forum - Yanolja motel booking foreigner)

Q: Can I sleep at a jjimjilbang (찜질방)?
A: Yes! Jjimjilbangs are 24-hour Korean bathhouses with sleeping areas. Entry fee ₩10,000–₩18,000 (as of 2025), and you can sleep in the common area (heated floor, provided mats/pillows). Perfect for a budget night. Expect: gender-separated bathing areas (fully nude, no swimsuits), then shared sauna/sleep zone (wear the provided shorts + t-shirt). Top picks: Siloam Sauna (Seoul Station), Spa Land Centum City (Busan). Lockers provided. Bring toiletries.
Source: lonelyplanet.com/articles/first-time-korean-bathhouse (entry ₩7,000–₩20,000); creatrip.com (Korean jjimjilbang guide)

Q: Do I need to show my passport at check-in?
A: Yes — by Korean law, all accommodation must register guests with their passport or ARC number. This applies to hotels, motels, guesthouses, hanok stays, and Airbnbs. It's routine, not suspicious. Just have your passport ready.
Source: Korea Immigration Service (hikorea.go.kr) — Foreigner Registration Act; general hotel practice confirmed across accommodation booking sites

Q: Is Airbnb legal in Korea in 2025?
A: Yes, but regulated. Legitimate listings must display a business registration number in the listing. Major issues: some buildings ban short-term rentals in their bylaws — if a listing tells you to "say you're a friend" or avoid the front desk, it's likely illegal and risky (cancellation with no recourse). Stick to superhosts with registration numbers visible. Booking.com and Agoda are safer bets for short stays.
Source: reddit.com/r/koreatravel (Airbnb registration requirements); bambooroutes.com (Airbnb regulations Korea 2025-2026); Korea Tourism Organization

### 🍜 Food

Q: How do I read Korean menus if I don't speak Korean?
A: Use Google Translate camera mode — point your phone at the menu and it overlays English in real time. Papago (Naver's app) is even better for Korean-to-English (more accurate than Google for Korean). For wall menus with no pictures, look for "인기메뉴" (popular menu) or just point at what others are eating. In tourist-heavy areas (Myeongdong, Hongdae, Itaewon), most restaurants have English or picture menus — just ask "English menu?" (영어 메뉴 있어요? yeong-eo menyu isseoyo?).
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/contentsView.do?vcontsId=140727 (Korean restaurant culture); papago.naver.com (official app)

Q: Is it weird to eat alone in Korea?
A: Not anymore. "Honbap" (혼밥, eating alone) is a mainstream trend now. Best solo-friendly spots: gimbap (김밥) shops, guksu (국수, noodle) restaurants, food courts, convenience stores, and franchise chains. Avoid: Korean BBQ (most require 2+ servings) and dakgalbi (닭갈비) places. Look for "1인분 가능" (one person portion available) on the sign. If the restaurant looks half-empty at lunch, you're fine solo.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/contentsView.do?vcontsId=140727 (Korean dining culture); korealocally.com/korea-solo-traveler-food-guide/

Q: Can I use food delivery apps without a Korean card?
A: Baemin (배민) mostly needs a Korean card or phone number. The best workaround: Shuttle Delivery (셔틀) — English interface, accepts foreign credit cards, no Korean phone required. It covers major Seoul zones (Gangnam, Mapo, Jongno, etc.), Busan (Haeundae, Gwanganri, Seomyeon), and Daegu. Coupang Eats requires prepayment in-app and does not support "pay at door" (현장결제), so a Korean card is needed there too. For late-night cravings: convenience stores are 24/7 and you can heat everything on-site.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/contentsView.do?vcontsId=226767 (Shuttle Delivery, May 2025); biz.chosun.com/en/en-retail/2025/07/29/K6W5BDAAFJGQ3MATLYDLQLIVJY/ (Coupang Eats no in-person payment, Jul 2025)

Q: How do I heat food at a convenience store?
A: Grab your food, bring it to the counter, and say "데워 주세요" (de-wo ju-se-yo = please heat this). The cashier will microwave it and hand it back. Every convenience store (GS25, CU, 7-Eleven, emart24) has a microwave counter. Some stores have self-service microwaves — look for the "전자레인지" sign. Don't microwave foil packages — peel those off first.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/contentsView.do?vcontsId=140727 (Korean restaurant tips); chisu-mom.com/expressions/korean-convenience-store/

Q: How do I make restaurant reservations in Korea as a foreigner?
A: Catch Table (캐치테이블) has an English app and accepts reservations with just an email — no Korean number needed. For Naver reservations, you'll need a Korean phone number (tough for tourists). Alternative: ask your hotel concierge to book for you, or call the Korea Travel Hotline (1330) — they'll make reservations for you in Korean for free. For super popular spots (Born & Bred, Mingles), book weeks ahead.

Q: Do I need to tip in Korea?
A: No. Tipping does not exist in Korea. Not in restaurants, taxis, bars, or hotels. Leaving money on the table will result in someone chasing you down to return it. The bill IS the final price — service is included. The only exception: high-end hotel bellhops sometimes expect ₩1,000–₩2,000, but this is rare.

Q: How do I get more side dishes (banchan)? Is water free?
A: Banchan (반찬) are unlimited and free — just ask "반찬 더 주세요" (banchan deo ju-se-yo). Many restaurants now have a self-serve banchan bar — look for the station with kimchi, pickled radish, and other sides. Water is always free and self-serve — find the water dispenser (usually labeled "정수기" or just look for a silver machine). Press the call button on your table if you need the server.
Source: koreabycar.com/journal/korean-restaurant-etiquette

### 💳 Money & Payments

Q: Where should I exchange money for the best rate?
A: Myeongdong currency exchange booths offer the best rates — often 1–3% better than banks and 3–5% better than Incheon Airport. Strategy: exchange ₩50,000–₩100,000 at the airport (enough for transport + first meal), then do your main exchange in Myeongdong. Bring crisp USD $100 bills for the absolute best rate. Chinese Embassy area (Euljiro) has competitive rates too. Exchange booths close around 7–8pm.
Source: mytriptip.com/basics/korea-money-guide/; ohmyseoul.page/en/guides/korea-money-exchange-guide

Q: Does Wise/Revolut work at Korean ATMs?
A: Yes, but with caveats. Wise: good rates on weekdays (near mid-market), but ~1.5% markup on weekends. Revolut: similar, with weekend surcharges. Best ATMs for foreign cards: SC제일은행 (Standard Chartered) — ₩0 withdrawal fee for some cards, Woori Bank 글로벌ATM (₩3,600 fee), and GS25 convenience store ATMs (₩3,000–₩4,000 fee). Max withdrawal ₩1,000,000/day. Always choose "CREDIT" or "CHECKING" — never "CASH ADVANCE."
Source: discoverrealkorea.com/exchange-money-in-korea/

Q: Why is my foreign card getting declined in Korea?
A: Common culprits: traditional markets (cash or local card only), small mom-and-pop restaurants, PC bangs, and some online payment gateways. Korea uses local payment processors that sometimes reject foreign-issued cards. Fixes: carry ₩50,000–₩100,000 cash as backup, try a different card (Visa/Mastercard most accepted, Amex less so), and get a NAMANE card — a prepaid debit card for foreigners available at airport kiosks (no ID verification needed).
Source: shop.namanecard.com

Q: What's a NAMANE or Wowpass card and should I get one?
A: NAMANE: prepaid card you buy at airport kiosks (₩5,000 card fee). No ID verification needed. Load it with foreign currency or cash, use it everywhere — transit (T-Money function), restaurants, shops, even online. You can customize the card with your own photo. Wowpass: similar prepaid card but with a dedicated app for foreign currency top-up at better exchange rates. Both are great if your foreign card keeps getting declined.
Source: shop.namanecard.com; klook.com (NAMANE card)

### 🗣️ Basic Korean

Q: What are the essential Korean phrases for eating out?
A: Here are the 10 you'll actually use:
1. "이거 주세요" (i-geo ju-se-yo) = this, please (point at menu/item)
2. "계산해 주세요" (gye-san-hae ju-se-yo) = check/bill please
3. "카드 돼요?" (ka-deu dwae-yo?) = do you take card?
4. "포장해 주세요" (po-jang-hae ju-se-yo) = to-go please
5. "데워 주세요" (de-wo ju-se-yo) = please heat this
6. "물 주세요" (mul ju-se-yo) = water please
7. "반찬 더 주세요" (ban-chan deo ju-se-yo) = more side dishes please
8. "잘 먹겠습니다" (jal meok-get-seum-ni-da) = I'll eat well (said before eating)
9. "잘 먹었습니다" (jal meo-geot-seum-ni-da) = I ate well (after eating, polite)
10. "맛있어요" (ma-sit-sseo-yo) = it's delicious!
Source: inmykorea.com/basic-korean-phrases-travel-korea/; verified via english.visitkorea.or.kr (general Korean phrases)

Q: What Korean phrases do I need for taxis?
A:
- Destination: "[place] 가 주세요" ([place] ga ju-se-yo) = take me to [place]
- Receipt: "영수증 주세요" (yeong-su-jeung ju-se-yo)
- Stop here: "여기 세워 주세요" (yeo-gi se-wo ju-se-yo)
- Turn left/right: "왼쪽/오른쪽" (oen-jjok/o-reun-jjok)
- Go straight: "직진" (jik-jin)
Pro tip: show the driver your destination in Korean on Naver Map — much easier than pronunciation.

Q: What do I say in an emergency?
A:
- Help!: "도와주세요!" (do-wa-ju-se-yo!)
- Hospital: "병원" (byeong-won)
- Police: "경찰" (gyeong-chal)
- I need a doctor: "의사 필요해요" (ui-sa pil-yo-hae-yo)
- Fire!: "불이야!" (bu-ri-ya!)
For tourists: 1330 (Tourism Helpline) speaks English 24/7. Just say "English please" and they'll connect you.

### 🚨 Emergency & Health

Q: What are the emergency numbers in Korea?
A:
- 112: Police (English interpretation available 24/7)
- 119: Fire + Ambulance (English available)
- 1330: Korea Travel Helpline — 24/7, free, English + 7 languages (Korean, English, Japanese, Chinese, Russian, Vietnamese, Thai, Indonesian). Use them for directions, translation, restaurant bookings, lost items — not just emergencies.
- 1345: Immigration Contact Center for visa/residency questions (hikorea.go.kr)
- 182: Lost & Found hotline (LOST112)
Save these in your phone. 1330 is the single most useful number for tourists.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/infoHtmlView.do?vcontsId=140042 (Emergency Situations page, verified 2026)

Q: Where can I find an English-speaking doctor or hospital in Seoul?
A: Major hospitals have International Clinics with English-speaking staff:
- Samsung Medical Center (삼성서울병원): Irwon-dong, Gangnam — International Health Center, +82-2-3410-0200
- Asan Medical Center (서울아산병원): Pungnap-dong, Songpa — International Healthcare Center, +82-2-3010-5001
- Severance Hospital (세브란스병원): Sinchon — International Health Care Center, +82-2-2228-5800
- Seoul National University Hospital (서울대병원): Jongno — International Clinic, +82-2-2072-0505 (emergency: +82-10-8831-2890)
All accept international insurance. For minor issues, visit a local clinic (의원, ui-won) — they're everywhere, cheap (₩10,000–₩30,000 walk-in), and many doctors speak basic medical English.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/infoHtmlView.do?vcontsId=140042 (verified hospital listings 2026); snuh.org/global/en/find/findDeptView.do?hsp_cd=1&dp_code=IHC (SNUH IHC page); eng.amc.seoul.kr (Asan official site)

Q: What medicine can I buy without a prescription at a Korean pharmacy?
A: Korean pharmacies (약국, yak-guk) sell basic OTC meds without prescription. Know these brands: Tylenol (타이레놀, ₩2,000–₩5,000) for headache/fever; Gelfos (겔포스, ₩5,000–₩8,000) for stomach relief; Pancold (판콜, ₩3,000–₩5,000) for cold symptoms; Salonpas (살롱파스) or Sinsinpas (신신파스) for muscle pain patches (파스). Look for the green or red cross sign (약). Describe symptoms — pharmacists are trained and many speak basic English. Antibiotics require prescription by law.
Source: blog.southofseoul.net/otc-medicine/; okrecruiting.com/your-guide-to-korean-pharmacies-otc-meds-for-native-english-teachers/; korepharm.com/collections/pain-relief

Q: Do I need travel insurance for Korea?
A: Not legally required for visa-free entry (K-ETA countries). But strongly recommended — a basic clinic visit costs ₩10,000–₩30,000, but an ER visit + tests can hit ₩500,000+. Korean hospitals upfront-charge uninsured patients. World Nomads, SafetyWing, and AXA are popular options for Korea coverage — $30–$60 for a week trip. Check if your credit card includes travel insurance — many premium cards do.
Source: k-eta.go.kr (no insurance requirement for K-ETA); nhis.or.kr/english (NHIS info); general travel insurance guidance

Q: I lost something on the subway — what do I do?
A: Don't panic — Korea has one of the world's best lost & found systems. Visit LOST112.go.kr (National Police Lost & Found Portal, English available). For subway: each subway line has its own lost & found office. Seoul Metro Line 1–8: call 1577-1234 or visit the office at the terminal station. Items are held for 7 days before going to police. Know the station name, line number, time, and item description. Most items are found — the return rate is remarkably high.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/infoHtmlView.do?vcontsId=140042 (LOST112: +82-2-182); lost112.go.kr/manyLanguage.do?langType=en; english.visitseoul.net/lost-and-found
