## 추가 Q&A — Traveler

### Getting Around (기존 6Q → +4Q)
- Does Google Maps work in Korea?
  > No. Google Maps cannot show walking or transit directions in Korea due to map data regulations. Download Naver Map (detailed) or KakaoMap (visual/intuitive) before you land. KakaoMap has English mode; Naver Map's English is partial but usable.
- What is the Climate Card and is it worth it?
  > ₩65,000/month for unlimited subway + bus + public bike (따릉이). Since March 2025, you can buy and reload with foreign credit/debit cards. Worth it if you're staying 2+ weeks in Seoul and riding daily. Buy at subway station kiosks.
- What time does the last subway run?
  > Last trains leave around 11:30 PM–12:30 AM depending on the line and direction, but the last train heading OUT of central Seoul often departs earlier than you expect. Check the last train time at your departure station on Naver Map before going out.
- Does Apple Pay work on the Seoul subway?
  > Yes, as of 2026. iPhone users can load transit credit via the MobileTmoney app into Apple Wallet (requires a Mastercard). Tap your iPhone at the subway gate like a T-Money card.

### Accommodation (기존 4Q → +3Q)
- What is a 한옥 (hanok) stay and how do I book one?
  > A hanok is a traditional Korean house — ondol floor heating, wooden beams, courtyard. Bukchon Hanok Village (Seoul) and Jeonju Hanok Village have the most options. Book via Airbnb or Booking.com. Expect ₩80,000–₩200,000/night. Not a budget option but a cultural experience.
- Can a foreigner stay in a 고시원 (gosiwon)?
  > Yes. Gosiwons are tiny furnished rooms (4–8㎡) with shared bathroom/kitchen, ₩250,000–₩450,000/month. No deposit required. Short-term (weekly) stays are possible. Popular with budget travelers and students. Quality varies wildly — check photos carefully.
- Are there any areas I should avoid staying in Seoul?
  > Seoul is very safe overall. No genuine no-go zones. That said: Itaewon Hamilton Hotel area gets extremely crowded on weekend nights (noise, crowds). For first-timers, Hongdae, Myeongdong, or Gangnam give easy subway access to everything.

### Money & Payments (기존 4Q → +3Q)
- Does Apple Pay / Google Pay work at Korean stores?
  > Apple Pay works at most major retailers and convenience stores as of 2024–2025, but acceptance varies — small restaurants and traditional markets often don't support it. Google Pay acceptance is more limited. Always carry some cash or a WOWPASS as backup.
- Can I use my foreign card at convenience stores?
  > Most GS25, CU, and 7-Eleven locations accept Visa/Mastercard. However, foreign cards are occasionally declined at older card terminals. If declined, try inserting instead of tapping, or use cash. Self-checkout machines at large marts generally work fine.
- How much cash should I carry daily?
  > ₩50,000–₩100,000 is enough for most days. You'll need cash for: traditional markets (시장), small local restaurants, temple entry fees, and some jjimjilbangs. Everywhere else — convenience stores, chains, taxis — accepts card.

### Emergency & Health (기존 4Q → +3Q)
- Can I bring my prescription medication into Korea?
  > Stimulants (ADHD medication including Adderall, Ritalin) are BANNED in Korea — even with a prescription. Bring a doctor's letter and pharmacy receipt for any controlled substances; check with the Korean Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (mfds.go.kr) before traveling. OTC-level medications are generally fine.
- What should I do if I get sick without travel insurance?
  > Korea has cheap walk-in clinics (의원). A doctor visit costs ₩10,000–₩30,000 without insurance. For a prescription, add ₩5,000–₩15,000 at the pharmacy. Emergency room at a public hospital runs ₩50,000–₩150,000 for basic treatment. Always get travel insurance — Korean private hospital bills add up fast.
- Is tap water safe to drink in Korea?
  > Technically yes — Seoul tap water meets WHO standards. But most Koreans don't drink it straight (taste/trust issues), and most accommodations have a water purifier (정수기). Bottled water is ₩800–₩1,500 at any convenience store.

## 추가 Q&A — Digital Nomad

### Banking (기존 4Q → +4Q)
- Can I use Kakao Bank or Toss as a foreigner?
  > Kakao Bank requires a Korean phone number AND ARC. Toss Bank requires the same. Neither works with a tourist visa. For nomads on D-10/F-1-D: KEB Hana (외국인 전담 창구) and Shinhan are the most foreigner-friendly for opening a real account.
- How do I receive international wire transfers without a Korean account?
  > Wise (transferwise.com) lets you receive KRW with a Korean bank account number they provide — no Korean account needed. Payoneer works similarly. Both are widely used by freelancers in Korea.
- What happens to my Korean bank account when I leave Korea?
  > Accounts stay open but may become dormant after 1 year of no transactions. Online banking may lock after your ARC/visa expires. Before leaving, either close the account in person or switch to a non-resident account (비거주자 계좌) — call your bank's foreigner help line.
- Which ATMs charge the lowest fees for foreign cards?
  > SC제일은행 (Standard Chartered): lowest fee, ~₩3,000. Woori Bank 글로벌ATM: ₩3,600. GS25/CU ATMs: ₩3,000–4,000 + your home bank's fee. Avoid airport Citibank ATMs — fees are higher. Withdraw larger amounts less frequently to minimize per-transaction fees.

### Tax & Visa (기존 4Q → +4Q)
- What is the D-10 visa and who qualifies?
  > D-10 (Job Seeker) visa is for those looking for employment or starting a business in Korea. Valid 6 months, extendable to 2 years. Requires proof of qualification (degree + work experience). Does NOT allow freelance income — you must be actively job seeking. Many nomads misuse this; if audited, it's a problem.
- Can I work remotely for a foreign company on a tourist visa?
  > Legally gray area. Korean law prohibits "employment activities" without a work visa, but remote work for a foreign employer (no Korean clients, no Korean-source income) is generally tolerated. The F-1-D (Digital Nomad) visa exists precisely to legalize this — highly recommended for stays over 90 days.
- Do I pay Korean income tax as a digital nomad?
  > If you stay 183+ days in a calendar year, Korea considers you a tax resident and taxes your worldwide income. Under 183 days: only Korean-source income is taxed. The F-1-D visa income requirement (~₩104.8M KRW/year based on 2025 GNI ×2) suggests the government expects you're earning abroad — but consult a tax advisor if in doubt.
- How do I get the F-1-D digital nomad visa?
  > Apply at a Korean consulate/embassy in your home country (cannot apply from within Korea). Requirements: passport, proof of income (₩104.8M KRW/year ≈ $73,710 USD based on 2025 GNI ×2), private health insurance (₩100M+ coverage), employment contract or business registration. Processing: 2–4 weeks. Valid 1 year, renewable once.

### Housing (기존 3Q → +3Q)
- What is a 보증금 (deposit) and how much do I need?
  > Wolse (월세) requires a deposit of ₩1M–₩10M + monthly rent. Officetels typically ask ₩3M–₩10M deposit + ₩600,000–₩1,200,000/month. Short-stay furnished options (Enkostay, 33m2, Stay B) require much smaller or no deposits — better for nomads staying under 3 months.
- Which neighborhoods are best for digital nomads in Seoul?
  > Hongdae/Mapo: young, cheap, tons of cafes. Seongsu: trendy, lots of coworking. Gangnam: expensive but fast wifi everywhere, business district. Yeonnam/Mangwon: quiet, walkable, indie cafes. Avoid Myeongdong/Itaewon for living — tourist pricing.
- Is subletting illegal in Korea?
  > Yes. Korean law prohibits subletting without landlord consent. Most Airbnb-style short-term rentals operate in a legal gray area. If you use platforms like Enkostay or share houses — these are legally registered as guesthouses or serviced residences. Always ask for the 숙박업 등록번호 (accommodation business registration number).

### SIM & Internet (기존 3Q → +3Q)
- What's the difference between a tourist SIM and a regular SIM?
  > Tourist SIMs (KT, SK, LG roaming SIMs at the airport): data-only or limited calls, 30-day max, no Korean number. Regular SIMs: requires ARC + Korean bank account, gives you a real Korean number. For nomads staying 1–3 months: tourist eSIM (Airalo, Roaming Man) works fine. For 3+ months: get a regular SIM with your ARC.
- Does my home country eSIM work in Korea?
  > Most international eSIMs (Airalo, Holafly, Nomad) work well in Korea with 4G/LTE speeds. 5G is available but typically requires a Korean carrier SIM. Check your eSIM provider's Korea coverage before buying — some throttle speeds after 1–3GB/day.
- What internet speed can I expect at cafes and coworking spaces?
  > Korea has some of the world's fastest internet. Convenience store cafes: 50–100 Mbps typical. Dedicated coworking spaces: 100–1000 Mbps guaranteed, often with backup lines. Even small neighborhood cafes usually have reliable wifi. Starbucks/chain cafes: 50–100 Mbps but often congested.

## 추가 Q&A — Student

### ARC (기존 4Q → +3Q)
- What happens if my ARC application is delayed?
  > You can use your application receipt (외국인등록 사실증명) as a temporary ID for most purposes, including opening a bank account at some banks. However, you CANNOT apply for a part-time work permit until you have the physical ARC. Delays of 2–6 weeks are common at semester start — apply the same week you arrive.
- Do I need to update my ARC when I move?
  > Yes. You must report an address change to the local immigration office (출입국관리소) within 15 days of moving. Failure to do so is technically a violation. You can do it online via HiKorea (hikorea.go.kr) — takes 5 minutes.
- Can I travel abroad and return to Korea during my studies?
  > Yes, your student visa (D-2) allows multiple re-entries. No re-entry permit needed. Just make sure your visa hasn't expired and your university enrollment is still valid. If your visa expires while you're abroad, you'll need to renew it at a Korean consulate before returning.

### Part-time Work (기존 4Q → +4Q)
- How do I apply for a part-time work permit (아르바이트 허가)?
  > Step 1: Complete one full semester with GPA 2.0+. Step 2: Get your school's signature on the part-time confirmation form (학교 확인서). Step 3: Apply online at HiKorea (hikorea.go.kr) or in person at your local immigration office. Processing: instant in-person, 1–2 weeks online. Fee: ₩30,000.
- How many hours can I work per week as a D-2 student?
  > 25 hours/week max during semesters. Unlimited during official summer/winter breaks (July–August, January–February). Manufacturing jobs are prohibited year-round. Delivery platform work (배달), private tutoring (과외), and door-to-door sales are also prohibited.
- Where do international students actually find part-time jobs?
  > Job Korea (잡코리아), Albamon (알바몬), and Craigslist Seoul for Korean-language jobs. Craigslist Seoul and Facebook groups (Seoul Expats, Korea Job Board) for English-oriented roles. University international offices post language exchange tutor roles. Convenience store (편의점) jobs are easiest to get with minimal Korean.
- What's the minimum wage in Korea and how do I get paid?
  > 2025 minimum wage: ₩10,030/hour. Employers must pay via bank transfer (not cash) for part-time workers. Get a Korean bank account before starting any job. Keep payslips — you'll need them for tax filing and sometimes for visa renewal.

### Housing (기존 4Q → +3Q)
- How do I avoid housing scams as a foreign student?
  > Red flags: landlord who won't meet in person, deposit amount that seems too low, pressure to sign quickly, no official 전세/월세 contract. Always use a licensed real estate agent (공인중개사 — look for the official certification). Check the building's 등기부등본 (property register) online at iros.go.kr before paying any deposit.
- What is a 고시원 (gosiwon) really like?
  > Tiny room (4–8㎡), bed, desk, sometimes a small fridge. Shared bathroom, kitchen, washing machine. Bills included. ₩250,000–₩450,000/month. No deposit or minimal (₩100,000–₩300,000). Best for: short-term while finding better housing, or if you're on a very tight budget. Not ideal for long-term comfort — walls are thin, rooms are very small.
- Can I live off-campus as a first-year international student?
  > Depends on your university. Most Korean universities give international students priority for on-campus dorms in year 1. Dorm applications open 6–10 weeks before semester — apply immediately. If you miss the dorm deadline or don't get in, goshiwons near campus are the fastest backup option.

### Health Insurance (기존 4Q → +2Q)
- Does NHIS cover dental and vision?
  > Dental: basic extractions and fillings covered (~50% of cost). Braces, implants, whitening — NOT covered. Vision: eye exams partially covered; glasses and contacts are NOT covered. For dental work beyond basics, negotiate directly — many clinics offer foreigner-friendly packages. Dental care is significantly cheaper than the US/UK even without insurance.
- What if I need to see a doctor but don't speak Korean?
  > Major university hospitals (서울대병원, 세브란스, 삼성서울병원) have international clinics with English-speaking staff — expect longer waits and higher fees (₩50,000–₩150,000 for consultation). Alternatively, use the 1339 health helpline for English guidance. The app "GoodDoc" lists English-speaking doctors near you.

### Banking (기존 4Q → +2Q)
- How do I send money home from Korea?
  > Wise (transferwise.com): best rates, lowest fees, 1–2 business days. Available once you have a Korean bank account. Western Union: available at post offices (우체국), no Korean account needed but fees are high. Kakao Pay International Transfer: convenient if you use Kakao Bank, limited countries. Avoid airport currency exchange for large amounts — rates are poor.
- What happens to my bank account when I graduate and leave Korea?
  > Close it in person before leaving, or it may become dormant. Any remaining balance can be withdrawn as foreign currency at the bank. Alternatively, transfer to Wise and keep the KRW balance there. Don't leave money in a Korean account you can't access after returning home.
