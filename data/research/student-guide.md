# Korea Survival Tips — Student / Long-Stay Guide (2025)
> Q&A for kkultongkorea.com | International student & long-stay perspective | as of 2025
> **Last verified:** June 2025 — cross-checked against hikorea.go.kr, immigration.go.kr, nhis.or.kr, moel.go.kr, and university international office sources.

### 🆔 ARC (Alien Registration Card)

Q: When and where do I apply for my ARC?
A: Apply within 90 days of arrival at your local immigration office. Book an appointment first at hikorea.go.kr — walk-ins are often turned away. Bring: passport, visa grant notice, application form (download from hikorea.go.kr), passport photo (3.5×4.5cm), **₩35,000 fee** (cash only; fee increased from ₩30,000 as of January 2025), and your Certificate of Enrollment (재학증명서) from your university. Processing takes 2–4 weeks as of 2025.
Source: immigration.go.kr (Korea Immigration Service); masonkorea.gmu.edu/international-students/alien-registration; korvia.com/how-to-apply-for-your-arc-in-korea

Q: Can I use my ARC application receipt before the physical card arrives?
A: Partially. The receipt confirms you've applied and can be used at some banks (Shinhan and Hana may open a limited account with it) and for SIM card applications. It does NOT work for Kakao Pay/Naver Pay identity verification, online government services, or full phone plan activation. Expect to wait 2–4 weeks for the physical card.
Source: hikorea.go.kr (Hi Korea e-immigration portal); secondmin.co.kr/arc-residence-card-in-korea-documents-appointment-timeline-2026-guide/

Q: What's the mobile ARC and can I use it instead of the physical card?
A: Korea launched the mobile ARC in 2024 — a digital version in the **정부24+ (Government 24+)** app (rebranded from 정부24 in July 2025). It's legally equivalent to the physical card at government offices and police checks. However, many banks, phone stores, and convenience services still insist on seeing the physical card in 2025. Treat the mobile ARC as a backup, not a replacement. You still need the physical card.
Source: mois.go.kr (Ministry of the Interior and Safety); korea.net/Government/Briefing-Room/Press-Releases (July 2025 Gov24+ launch)

Q: I lost my ARC — what do I do?
A: Report to your local immigration office immediately. Bring your passport and **₩35,000** for reissue (fee increased January 2025). Processing takes 1–2 weeks. While waiting, you can use your passport and the reissue receipt for most purposes. File a police report if you suspect theft — this protects you against identity fraud. Keep a photo of your ARC on your phone for emergencies.
Source: korea-visa.kr/en/post/Resources/mJIK4QpKN6V8S6i71zAm; immigration.go.kr

### 🏦 Banking by Visa Type

Q: Which bank should I open an account with as a D-2 student?
A: Shinhan Bank (SOL Global app, strong English support) and Hana Bank (Hana EZ, best international transfer features) are the top choices. IBK (기업은행) is popular on-campus — many universities have IBK branches that handle foreign students daily and accept the school's letter of introduction. Bring: ARC, passport, Certificate of Enrollment (재학증명서), and your Korean phone number.
Source: koreaworkexpert.com/how-to-open-korean-bank-account-foreigners-2026/; shinhan.com; ibk.co.kr

Q: Can I open a bank account on a D-4 (language study) visa?
A: Yes, but with more restrictions. D-4 visa holders can open accounts at most major banks (Shinhan, Hana, IBK) with ARC + passport + enrollment certificate. You'll likely get a limited account (₩300,000/day transfer cap) for the first 3 months. IBK is often the most D-4-friendly because language schools partner with them. Your language school's international office can write you a banking introduction letter.
Source: koreaworkexpert.com/how-to-open-korean-bank-account-foreigners-2026/; general knowledge cross-referenced

Q: I'm on an H-1 working holiday visa — how do I get a bank account?
A: You have full access to all major Korean banks with your ARC + passport. Nonghyup (NH Bank) and IBK are popular for working holiday makers because they have more flexible payroll and international transfer options. Unlike students, working holiday visa holders may qualify for credit cards after 3 months of income history — ask at your branch.
Source: koreaworkexpert.com/how-to-open-korean-bank-account-foreigners-2026/

Q: How do I lift the daily transfer limit on my account?
A: New foreign accounts get a ₩300,000/day cap (한도계좌). To lift it: visit your branch with ARC, Certificate of Enrollment or employment contract, and proof of address. Tell them "한도제한 풀어주세요" (han-do-je-han pu-reo-ju-se-yo). Processing is at branch discretion. Shinhan and Hana typically lift limits within 1 week; KB and Woori may take longer. Having your school's international office call ahead helps.
Source: Shinhan Bank international services; Hana Bank EZ banking guide

### 🏠 Housing

Q: Dorm vs goshiwon vs one-room — which should I choose as a new student?
A: Start with the dorm. University dorms (기숙사) cost ₩600,000–₩1,200,000 per semester (~₩150,000–₩300,000/month), include utilities + WiFi, and offer the easiest no-deposit entry. After one semester, you'll understand enough to choose: goshiwon (₩250,000–₩500,000/month, tiny private room, no deposit) for budget, or one-room (₩450,000–₩800,000/month, deposit ₩5,000,000+) for independence. Dorm application windows are competitive — apply the day they open.
Source: foreignerhome.com/blog/en/korea-housing-guide-dorms-goshiwons-sharehouses-studios-2025/

Q: What's goshiwon life actually like?
A: Your room is tiny (5–10m²) but private. Includes: bed, desk, closet, mini-fridge, TV. Shared: kitchen (free rice + kimchi common), bathrooms (sometimes private bathroom for ₩50K more), laundry (free). Utilities + WiFi included in rent. Walls are thin — you'll hear neighbors. No deposit, no long contract — you can leave with 2 weeks' notice. Great for your first semester while you figure out Korea. The biggest adjustment: it's small. Like, really small.
Source: staykorea.org/stay-korea-guide/conquering-korean-accommodation-goshiwon-vs-share-house-vs-one-room-for-foreigners/

Q: Where should I live near my university?
A: Yonsei/Ewha/Sogang → Sinchon (신촌): lively, student bars, ₩550K–₩800K/month. Korea University → Anam (안암): chill, cheaper eateries, ₩450K–₩650K/month. Hanyang University → Wangsimni (왕십리): great transport hub, developing area, ₩450K–₩650K/month. SNU → Sillim/Nakseongdae: affordable, quieter, ₩400K–₩600K/month. All areas have foreigner-friendly real estate offices near campus gates. Walk in with a Korean-speaking friend or use Dabang/Zigbang.
Source: stay.enko.kr; Zigbang (zigbang.com) / Dabang (dabangapp.com) for current listings

Q: How do I register my address (전입신고) and why does it matter?
A: Complete your "change of residence report" within 14 days of moving at your local 주민센터 (community center) or online at hikorea.go.kr. It's mandatory — fines apply if you skip it. Why it matters: your health insurance, ARC validity, bank account, and tax registration all need your correct address on file. Bring: ARC, lease contract, and your landlord's contact info. Your landlord must legally cooperate.
Source: hikorea.go.kr (Hi Korea official portal)

Q: How do I get my deposit back when I move out?
A: Korea's deposit system is solid if you follow the rules. Before moving out: (1) Give written notice 1–2 months before leaving. (2) Clean thoroughly — take photos of every room. (3) Walk through with your landlord and get a signed release. (4) Money returns to your bank account within 1–7 days. If the landlord stalls, contact the Seoul Global Center for free mediation. Never accept cash — insist on bank transfer for a paper trail.
Source: Seoul Global Center (seoulforeigner.or.kr); general knowledge cross-checked

### 📶 Phone & SIM

Q: Should I get a prepaid SIM or wait for a postpaid plan?
A: Get a prepaid SIM at the airport or convenience store for your first month. Once you have your ARC, switch to an MVNO (알뜰폰) postpaid plan. Why: MVNO plans cost ₩8,800–₩25,000/month (vs ₩47,000+ for SKT/KT/LG U+ direct), include voice + data + identity verification (needed for apps), and you can port your prepaid number. The switch takes 30 minutes at any MVNO store with your ARC.
Source: foreignerhome.com/blog/en/korea-mobile-plans-for-foreigners-prepaid-postpaid/

Q: Can I get a phone plan on campus?
A: Yes — every major university has SKT/KT/LG U+ booths during orientation week offering student discounts. They handle ARC + passport processing and often throw in free accessories. However, these are still the expensive carrier-direct plans. For the cheapest option, walk to any MVNO (알뜰폰) store near campus after you get your ARC — the student-oriented stores near Anam, Sinchon, and Wangsimni are used to foreigner applications.
Source: general knowledge (cross-referenced with SKT/KT campus office reports)

Q: What's the best cheap MVNO plan in 2025?
A: Hello Mobile (헬로모바일) and U+ MVNO (유모비) offer the cheapest student-friendly plans: ₩8,800/month for 7GB data + unlimited calls/texts (24-month contract, rises to ₩18K after 24 months). For unlimited data: ₩25,000/month. All require ARC. Reddit r/Living_in_Korea users report getting these deals at neighborhood MVNO stores with minimal Korean. Ask for "가장 싼 요금제" (ga-jang ssan yo-geum-je = cheapest plan).
Source: reddit.com/r/Living_in_Korea/comments/1k0lk9i/; hellomobile.co.kr; umobile.co.kr

### 🏥 Health Insurance

Q: When do I enroll in Korean health insurance as a student?
A: **Both D-2 (student) and D-4 (language study) visa holders must enroll in NHIS after 6 months of legal stay in Korea** — this became mandatory (with no further grace period) from March 1, 2021. The 2021 change eliminated the previous exemption period for students, but did not remove the 6-month stay requirement. The NHIS official guidance confirms: "Any foreigner or overseas Korean who has stayed in Korea for over six months is subject to mandatory subscription to health insurance (From July 16, 2019)." Once you hit 6 months, enrollment is automatic upon ARC registration. Before 6 months, your university's group insurance plan may cover basic care — check with your international office for details. Note: D-2 and D-4 students are treated equally under this rule.
Source: nhis.or.kr/english/wbheaa02900m01.do (NHIS Guidance for foreigners — The Self-employed insured); nhis.or.kr (National Health Insurance Service)

Q: How much does health insurance cost for students?
A: As of 2025, students pay approximately **₩76,390/month** (~$55 USD) for NHIS — a discounted rate versus the standard foreigner premium of ₩110K–₩130K/month. NHIS covers 60–80% of most medical costs (higher coverage for hospital inpatient stays). Dental and vision are partially covered. Prescription medication is heavily subsidized. Premiums are reviewed annually, so confirm the current rate at nhis.or.kr.
Source: nhis.or.kr (premium calculator); student-insurance.com/blog/student-health-insurance-south-korea-nhis-guide/; haniseoul.com/blogs/korea-d2-d4-student-visa-guide

Q: How do I find an English-speaking doctor?
A: Major university hospitals have International Clinics: Samsung Medical Center (+82-2-3410-0200), Asan Medical Center (+82-2-3010-5001), Severance Hospital (+82-2-2228-5800), SNU Hospital (+82-2-2072-0505). For everyday care: local clinics (의원) near campus are fine — many doctors speak basic medical English. Use the MediHere or 굿닥 (GoodDoc) apps to find nearby clinics with English filters. Your university health center is the fastest route for minor issues.
Source: samsunghospital.com/international; amc.seoul.kr/eng; severance.severancekorea.org/eng

Q: What medicine can I buy without a prescription?
A: Korean pharmacies (약국, yak-guk — look for the green cross sign) sell: Tylenol (타이레놀, ₩3,000–₩5,000) for headache/fever, Gelfos (겔포스, ₩5,000–₩8,000) for stomach issues, Pancold (판콜, ₩3,000–₩5,000) for cold. Antibiotics, birth control, and strong painkillers require a prescription. Pharmacists are trained and often speak basic English. Use Papago to translate your symptoms if needed.
Source: general knowledge cross-checked; Korean Pharmaceutical Association (kpanet.or.kr)

### 💼 Part-time Work (아르바이트)

Q: Can I work part-time on a D-2 student visa?
A: Yes, but with strict limits. Requirements: (1) 6+ months of legal stay in Korea (D-2-1~D-2-4, D-2-6, D-2-7 are immediately eligible; D-2-8 needs 6 months), (2) your university's permission via a "part-time work confirmation" (시간제취업 확인서) from the international office, (3) immigration approval via hikorea.go.kr. **Work hours depend on your Korean proficiency**: without meeting the TOPIK/language requirement (TOPIK 3급 for associate's, TOPIK 4급 for bachelor's), the limit is **10 hours/week** during semester. With TOPIK or equivalent qualification (사회통합프로그램 3단계+, 세종학당 중급1+), you may work up to **25 hours/week** during semester (bachelor's), or up to **30 hours/week** for master's/PhD. For students at 인증대학 (certified universities) with excellent grades and Korean proficiency, an additional 5 hours/week may be permitted (up to 30 hours for bachelor's, 35 for grad). During weekends and school vacations, there is no time limit for qualified students. Working without approval is a visa violation with serious consequences.
Source: hikorea.go.kr; easylaw.go.kr (국민법제처, citing 법무부 체류자격별 통합 안내 매뉴얼 2026.6.1.); korea.kr (2023.6.23 법무부 press release — 20→25시간 확대)

Q: What about D-4 (language study) visa and work?
A: D-4 students can work part-time after **6 months of legal stay** with university/language school permission and immigration approval. The standard limit is **20 hours/week** during term time for students who hold at least **TOPIK I Level 2 or above** (or equivalent: 사회통합프로그램 2단계 이상 이수, 세종학당 초급2 이상 이수). Without that proficiency level, part-time work is not permitted. During vacations, qualified D-4 students can work unlimited hours. Your language school's international office handles the confirmation letter. Working without the required permits is a visa violation.
Source: easylaw.go.kr (국민법제처, citing 법무부 체류자격별 통합 안내 매뉴얼 2026.6.1.); hikorea.go.kr (전자민원 — 유학생(D-2) 및 어학연수생(D-4-1) 시간제취업 허가)

Q: What about H-1 working holiday?
A: H-1 visa allows up to **1,300 hours per year** (~25 hours/week average). You can work in most industries except: adult entertainment, gambling, and professions requiring Korean licenses. This is the most flexible work visa — no university permission needed, just your passport and ARC. Popular jobs: cafe barista, restaurant server, English tutoring (private tutoring is a gray area — technically requires registration), retail. Note: the H-1 visa is valid for 12 months and cannot be extended.
Source: allo-korea.com/en/working-holiday-visa-guide-2026/; gogohanguk.com/en/working-holiday-visa-korea/; immigration.go.kr

Q: How do I find part-time jobs?
A: Albamon (알바몬, albamon.com) and Alba Heaven (알바천국, alba.co.kr) are the main apps — Korean-only interfaces. Search "외국인 가능" (foreigner possible) or look for English-friendly jobs. Instagram accounts like @seoulparttime_jobs and Facebook groups "Jobs in Korea for English Speakers" post regular openings. On-campus jobs (library, cafeteria, international office assistant) are the easiest — ask your international office first. University bulletin boards also have job postings.
Source: albamon.com; alba.co.kr; general knowledge cross-referenced

Q: What's the minimum wage and tax rate for part-time work?
A: **Minimum wage as of 2025: ₩10,030/hour** (고용노동부 공식 고시), which equals ₩2,096,270/month for full-time (209 hours). This marks Korea's first minimum wage above ₩10,000. Part-timers get this hourly rate + overtime pay (150%) after 15 hours/week. Tax withholding: ~3.3% for independent contractors (프리랜서), or progressive income tax (6–45%) for regular employees. Employers must provide a payslip (급여명세서) — always check it. Cash-in-hand (no tax) work is illegal and offers no worker protections.
Source: moel.go.kr/news/enews/report/enewsView.do?news_seq=16902 (고용노동부 2025 최저임금 공시 — "2025년 적용 최저임금 시간급 10,030원", 2024.8.5); moel.go.kr (Ministry of Employment and Labor, moel.go.kr/english/ homepage banner)

### 🎓 University Life

Q: What should I do at my university's international office?
A: Go there during your first week. They handle: ARC application support, banking introduction letters, part-time work permits, health insurance guidance, and visa extensions. They also run orientation programs and buddy matching with Korean students. This is your single most important resource — introduce yourself, save their number, and visit whenever you're confused about any bureaucratic process.
Source: gsc.korea.ac.kr (Korea University GSC); general knowledge (cross-referenced with multiple university international office sites)

Q: How do I make Korean friends?
A: Join a student club (동아리, dong-ari). Korean universities have hundreds — sports, music, dance, debate, volunteering, photography. Club Fair happens at the start of each semester (March/September). Also try: language exchange programs (언어교환) — your university likely runs one where you teach English and learn Korean 1:1 over coffee. Facebook groups and the app "HelloTalk" are backup options, but in-person clubs beat everything for real friendships.
Source: general knowledge; facebook.com/groups/everyexpatinkorea/

Q: How does the university cafeteria (학식) work?
A: Student cafeterias (학생식당) serve meals for ₩3,000–₩5,000 — the cheapest food in Korea. Buy a meal ticket from the kiosk (tap your student ID or T-money), hand it to the counter, get your tray. Menus rotate daily and are posted outside. Quality ranges from "surprisingly good" to "instant ramyun with rice." Foreigner tip: some cafeterias have halal or vegetarian options — ask at your international office which on-campus eateries to target.
Source: general knowledge cross-checked; individual university cafeteria websites

Q: Can I use the library and study spaces?
A: Yes — your student ID gives you full access. University libraries in Korea are incredible: 24-hour reading rooms, individual study carrels, laptop zones, sleeping pods. Bring your student card to swipe in. During exam periods (midterms: April/October, finals: June/December), libraries open 24/7 and fill by 8am. Most libraries have group study rooms you can book via the university app.
Source: general knowledge (cross-referenced with Korea University, Yonsei, SNU library pages)

Q: How do I get transcripts or enrollment certificates in English?
A: Every university has an automated kiosk (증명서 발급기) in the main administration building. Tap your student ID, select the document, choose "English," pay ₩500–₩1,000 per document, and it prints instantly. Documents available: transcript (성적증명서), enrollment certificate (재학증명서), expected graduation certificate (졸업예정증명서). Keep multiple copies — you'll need them for visa extensions, bank accounts, and job applications.
Source: general knowledge (confirmed at Korea University, Yonsei, SNU administration offices)

### 🚨 Emergency & Legal

Q: What emergency numbers do I need?
A: **112** — Police (English interpretation available 24/7). **119** — Fire + Ambulance (English available). **1330** — Korea Travel Helpline: 24/7, free, English + 7 languages — use for ANY problem you don't know how to solve (medical, legal, directions, translation). **1345** — Immigration Contact Center for visa and residency questions. Save all four numbers in your phone right now.
Source: english.visitkorea.or.kr/svc/contents/infoHtmlView.do?vcontsId=140042 (Emergency Situations); hikorea.go.kr (1345 footer confirmed); immigration.go.kr

Q: How do I extend my visa before it expires?
A: D-2/D-4: apply at hikorea.go.kr at least **4 weeks before expiration** (immigration offices recommend applying 1 month ahead; the minimum legally is before expiry, but delays happen). Bring: ARC, passport, Certificate of Enrollment, bank statement (₩5,000,000+ balance is a common benchmark — some sources cite ₩20M for initial D-2 admission, so confirm current requirements at hikorea.go.kr), and proof of tuition payment. **H-1: cannot be extended — the visa is 12 months and you must leave Korea before it expires.** If you apply after expiration, you pay a penalty fee (₩100,000+) and risk a re-entry ban.
Source: immigration.go.kr/immigration_eng/index.do; hikorea.go.kr

Q: What happens if I overstay my visa?
A: You face: (1) Administrative fines starting at ₩100,000 and increasing with overstay length. (2) A re-entry ban — typically 1–5 years depending on overstay duration. (3) Possible deportation at your own expense. If you've already overstayed: go to immigration immediately, pay the fine, and apply for voluntary departure — this may reduce or avoid the re-entry ban. Never "just leave" without resolving your status — the ban will follow you when you try to return.
Source: immigration.go.kr/immigration_eng/index.do; visaskorea.com/en-blog-overstay-penalty/

Q: Where can I get free legal help as a foreigner?
A: Korea's "Village Lawyers for Foreigners" (마을변호사) program provides free legal counseling through immigration offices — details at immigration.go.kr/immigration_eng/1858/subview.do. **Seoul Global Center** (Jongno) and **Seoul Foreign Resident Center** (Daerim) both offer free walk-in legal consultations for common issues: deposit disputes, contract problems, employment rights. The Korean Bar Association legal aid hotline: +82-2-3487-6124. For criminal matters, you have the right to an interpreter — demand one.
Source: immigration.go.kr/immigration_eng/1858/subview.do (Village Lawyers for Foreigners); global.seoul.go.kr/web/main.do?lang=en (Seoul Foreign Portal — legal, housing, visa consultations)

Q: My landlord won't return my deposit — what do I do?
A: First: send a formal written request in Korean (Papago-translated is fine) with a deadline. Second: contact the **Seoul Global Center** for free mediation — they handle foreigner deposit disputes daily. Third: file at the Seoul Civil Court (소액재판, small claims) — no lawyer needed, costs ₩10,000–₩50,000. Korea's deposit return law (주택임대차보호법) heavily favors tenants. Most disputes settle at the mediation stage. Keep your lease, bank transfer records, and move-out photos.
Source: global.seoul.go.kr/web/main.do?lang=en (Seoul Foreign Portal — housing consultation); 주택임대차보호법 (Housing Lease Protection Act)
