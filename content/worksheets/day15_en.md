# Day 15. Particles: What English Doesn't Have

## Day 14 Recap

What you learned in Day 14:
- Korean and English have different word order — Korean is SOV, the predicate always comes last
- Particles mark each word's role, which is why word order can be flexible
- Predicate slots: one-place (비가 와요) / two-place (나는 학생이에요) / three-place (친구가 나한테 문자를 보냈어요)

Today, we take a close look at those particles.

---

## English Goes Before. Korean Goes After.

Particles do a similar job to English prepositions — they mark what role a noun plays in the sentence.
The difference is position.

English has **prepositions**. The name gives it away — they go *before* (pre-) the noun.

```
I go  to    school.
       ↑
  preposition — before the noun
```

Korean has **particles** (조사 [jo-sa]). They go *after* the noun.

```
나는 학교  에  가요.
        ↑
  particle — after the noun
```

Similar role, opposite position:

```
English:   to  school     ← preposition comes first
Korean:    학교  에         ← particle comes after
```

And Korean particles do far more than English prepositions.
They also handle what English handles through word order — marking who is the subject and who is the object.

---

## Particles at a Glance

Here's a map of the most common Korean particles.
Notice how many have no English equivalent — that's because English uses word order to do the same job.

| Role | Particle | Example | English equivalent |
|------|----------|---------|--------------------|
| Subject (topic) | 은, 는 | 나**는** | — (handled by word order) |
| Subject (focus) | 이, 가 | 친구**가** | — (handled by word order) |
| Object | 을, 를 | 밥**을** | — (handled by word order) |
| Place (destination) | 에 | 학교**에** | to, at |
| Place (action) | 에서 | 학교**에서** | at, in |
| Direction / means | (으)로 | 버스**로** | by, with, to |
| Recipient (person) | 에게, 한테 | 친구**에게** | to |
| Together / listing | 와, 과, 하고 | 친구**하고** | with, and |
| Also (inclusion) | 도 | 나**도** | too, also |
| Only (limit) | 만 | 나**만** | only |
| Starting point | 부터 | 월요일**부터** | from |
| End point | 까지 | 금요일**까지** | until, to |

The difference between 은/는 and 이/가 gets its own day in Day 16. 에 vs 에서, (으)로, and the rest are in Day 17.

## Form Changes: 받침 Decides

Some particles come in two forms — same role, different shape — depending on whether the preceding word ends in a consonant (받침) or a vowel.

| Role | With 받침 | Without 받침 | Example |
|------|-----------|--------------|---------|
| Subject (topic) | 은 | 는 | 학생**은** / 나**는** |
| Subject (focus) | 이 | 가 | 선생님**이** / 고양이**가** |
| Object | 을 | 를 | 밥**을** / 커피**를** |
| Direction / means | 으로 | 로 | 지갑**으로** / 버스**로** |

Why two forms? Pure pronunciation comfort.

```
학생 + 는  →  학생는   →  ŋ followed by n — awkward
학생 + 은  →  학생은   →  flows naturally

나 + 을  →  나을   →  two vowels colliding — awkward
나 + 를  →  나를   →  natural
```

One exception for (으)로: when the 받침 is **ㄹ**, use **로** even though there is a 받침.

```
지하철 (받침 ㄹ)  →  지하철로    (으로 ✗)
버스   (no 받침)  →  버스로
지갑   (받침 ㅂ)  →  지갑으로
```

## 이다 — A Particle That Builds the Predicate

The most basic sentence pattern in Korean:

```
나는    학생이다.
I       am a student.
```

Break it down:

```
나는        학생이다
↑           ↑
Subject     Predicate (noun + 이다)
```

There's no verb in the predicate. No adjective either.
It's **a noun (학생) + a copular particle (이다)** — together they function as the predicate.

In English, "am" is a verb. In Korean, the particle **이다** does that job.

```
I    am    a student.
나는       학생이다.
           ↑
    particle attached to a noun (acts like "be")
```

Form by 받침:

```
학생 + 이다  →  학생이다          (받침 present)
의사 + 이다  →  의사이다 / 의사다  (no 받침 → 이 can be dropped)
```

해요체 — the most widely used polite register in everyday Korean. Day 19 covers the full speech-level system, but you'll see it naturally in examples from here on.

```
받침 present  →  이에요    학생이에요, 동물이에요
no 받침       →  예요      의사예요, 가수예요
```

---

## 아니다 — The Opposite of 이다, a Different Grammar Category

What's the opposite of 나는 학생이다?

```
나는 학생이다.          나는 학생이 아니다.
I am a student.    →   I am not a student.
```

The meaning flips — but the sentence structure changes completely:

```
나는  학생이다
↑     ↑
Subj  Predicate (noun + particle — merged)


나는  학생이  아니다
↑     ↑       ↑
Subj  Comp.   Predicate (adjective)
      ← 학생이 and 아니다 are separate
```

**이다**: a **particle** that attaches to a noun → 학생**이다** (merged)
**아니다**: a standalone **adjective** that acts as the predicate → 학생이 **아니다** (separate)

Because 아니다 is an adjective, it always goes at the end of the sentence.
And there's always a noun marked with 이/가 (the complement) right before it.

해요체:

```
이에요 / 예요    →  학생이에요, 의사예요
이 아니에요     →  학생이 아니에요  (complement has 받침 → 이)
가 아니에요     →  의사가 아니에요  (complement has no 받침 → 가)
```

## Day 15 Checklist

- [ ] English prepositions go **before** nouns; Korean particles go **after**
- [ ] Particles mark each word's role — the job English word order does
- [ ] 받침 present → 은/이/을/으로 / no 받침 → 는/가/를/로
- [ ] **ㄹ exception** — 받침 ㄹ still takes **로**, not 으로 (지하철로)
- [ ] **이다** — copular particle. Attaches to a noun to form the predicate (받침 → 이에요, no 받침 → 예요)
- [ ] **아니다** — adjective. Always at the end; always preceded by a noun + 이/가 complement
- [ ] 나는 학생이다 (noun + particle → predicate) vs 나는 학생이 아니다 (complement + adjective predicate)
