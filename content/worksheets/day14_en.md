# Day 14. Why Korean Sentences Are Different from English

## Series 2 Recap — How Words Are Built

Days 8–13 were all about how Korean words are formed.

```
Compound words       Two words merged into one         봄비, 손발, 눈물
Prefixes             Added to the front to modify      맨발, 늦잠, 날것
Suffixes             Added to the back to change form  먹기, 믿음
-하다/-되다           Noun → verb / passive             공부하다, 자동화되다
Sino-Korean suffixes One pattern, dozens of words      한국적, 가능성, 세계화
```

You know the words. Now it's time to learn how to build sentences with them.

---

## Before We Start: This Line

You're watching a K-drama and you hear this:

```
나는 너를 사랑해.
[na-neun / neo-reul / sa-rang-hae]
```

You finished Series 1 and 2 — you can read Hangul, and you know the words.

> 나는 → I  /  너를 → you  /  사랑해 → love

Three words, all familiar. But the order feels off.

In English: **I love you** — I, *love*, you.
In Korean: **나는 너를 사랑해** — I, you, *love*.

This isn't just a quirk of word order.
**Korean and English design sentences in fundamentally different ways.**

---

## Two Languages, Two Systems

English is an **SVO** language.

```
Subject  →  Verb   →  Object
   I     →  love   →   you
```

Position determines role:
- *I love you* → I'm the one who loves
- *You love me* → you're the one who loves

Change the order and the meaning changes. **Word order is the grammar.**

Korean is an **SOV** language.

```
Subject  →  Object  →  Verb(Predicate)
  나는   →   너를   →    사랑해
  (I)        (you)       (love)
```

```
English:  I      love    you
          S      V       O

Korean:   나는   너를    사랑해
          S      O       Predicate
```

Here's where it gets interesting: in Korean, you can scramble the word order and the meaning stays the same.

```
나는 너를 사랑해.   →  I love you.
너를 나는 사랑해.   →  I love you.  (just a different emphasis)
```

That's impossible in English. The reason it works in Korean? **Particles (조사).**

---

## Particles — Why Word Order Can Be Free

A particle is a small word that attaches directly to a noun and marks **what role that noun plays** in the sentence.

```
나는  =  나 + 는  →  marks "나" as the topic
너를  =  너 + 를  →  marks "너" as the object
```

In English, **word order** tells you who does what.
In Korean, **particles** do that job. Since every noun carries its role with it, the order can shift.

Here's a quick overview of the main particles:

| Role | Particle | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| Topic | 은, 는 | 나**는**, 선생님**은** |
| Subject | 이, 가 | 고양이**가**, 친구**가** |
| Object | 을, 를 | 밥**을**, 커피**를** |
| Place · destination | 에 | 학교**에** 가요 |
| Action location · starting point | 에서 | 학교**에서** 배워요 |
| Direction · means | (으)로 | 버스**로** 가요 |
| Target (person) | 에게, 한테 | 친구**에게**, 나**한테** |
| Together · listing | 와, 과, 하고 | 친구**하고** |
| Start · end point | 부터, 까지 | 월요일**부터** 금요일**까지** |

Each particle's nuances and usage get a full session in Day 15.

## Two Spacing Rules

Now that you know particles attach directly to nouns, spacing makes immediate sense.

Korean spaces words just like English does.

```
나는  너를  사랑해.
 ↑    ↑      ↑
word  word   word — space between each
```

But particles aren't independent words — they attach to the noun before them. So they get written with no space.

```
나는   =  나 + 는   →  written together (NO SPACE)
너를   =  너 + 를   →  written together
학교에 = 학교 + 에  →  written together
```

**Rule 1:** Word ↔ Word → space
**Rule 2:** Word ↔ Particle → no space

These two rules are the foundation. That said, Korean spacing has plenty of exceptions — even native speakers get them wrong regularly. Dependent nouns, auxiliary verbs, and counters are covered in a separate deep-dive resource.

## English Sentence Structure: The Five Patterns

English grammar classifies sentences into **five patterns** based on what comes after the verb.

| Pattern | Structure | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Pattern 1 | S + V | The bird sings. / She sleeps. |
| Pattern 2 | S + V + C (complement) | She became a doctor. / He is tall. |
| Pattern 3 | S + V + O (object) | I eat rice. / She reads books. |
| Pattern 4 | S + V + IO + DO | Mom gave me a gift. / He told her the truth. |
| Pattern 5 | S + V + O + OC (object complement) | They elected her leader. / I found it difficult. |

The verb (V) always follows the subject directly, and the elements after it determine the pattern.

---

## Korean Sentence Structure: Predicate Slots (서술어의 자릿수)

Korean doesn't use the five-pattern system. Instead, it classifies sentences by **predicate slots (서술어의 자릿수)** — how many elements a predicate needs to complete the sentence.

Two things work differently from English:
- The predicate always comes **at the very end** of the sentence (SOV, not SVO)
- Korean has a category called **required adverbials (필수적 부사어)** that doesn't exist in English

---

### One-Place Predicates

The subject alone is enough to complete the sentence. Similar to English Pattern 1 (S + V).

```
꽃이    피었어요.        The flower bloomed.
언니가   자요.           My older sister sleeps.
날씨가   좋아요.         The weather is nice.
고양이가  귀여워요.       The cat is cute.
하늘이   맑아요.         The sky is clear.
```

Both verbs (피었어요, 자요) and adjectives (좋아요, 귀여워요) can be one-place predicates.

---

### Two-Place Predicates

One additional element beyond the subject is needed. This breaks into three types based on the predicate.

---

**A. Subject + Object + Predicate**

Transitive verbs need an object — the thing receiving the action. Similar to English Pattern 3 (S + V + O). The elements are the same; only the **order** of object and predicate is reversed.

```
저는    커피를   마셔요.         I drink coffee.
나는    음악을   들어요.         I listen to music.
오빠가   드라마를  봐요.          My older brother watches dramas.
우리가   한국어를  배워요.        We learn Korean.
언니가   책을     읽어요.         My older sister reads books.
```

---

**B. Subject + Complement + Predicate (되다 / 아니다)**

Predicates expressing change or negation need a complement. Similar to English Pattern 2 (S + V + C).

```
물이     얼음이    됐어요.         Water became ice.
애벌레가  나비가    됐어요.         The caterpillar became a butterfly.
저는     학생이    아니에요.        I am not a student.
이건     커피가    아니에요.        This is not coffee.
언니가   선생님이  됐어요.         My older sister became a teacher.
```

The particles that attach to complements (이/가), and a full breakdown of 이다/아니다, are in Day 15.

---

**C. Subject + Required Adverbial + Predicate**

In English, adverbials are optional extras.

```
I went.         (grammatically complete on its own)
I went home.    (home is extra information)
```

In Korean, certain predicates **require** an adverbial to complete the sentence.

```
저는    학교에    가요.           I go to school.
나는    집에      왔어요.          I came home.
영희는  철수와    닮았어요.        Younghee resembles Cheolsu.
이건    저와      달라요.          This is different from me.
저는    집에      있어요.          I'm at home.
```

"저는 가요" — without *where*, the sentence feels incomplete. "학교에" has to be there.
This is a structure Korean has that English simply doesn't.

---

### Three-Place Predicates

Two additional elements beyond the subject are needed. Similar to English Pattern 4 (S + V + IO + DO).

```
할머니께서   우리에게   세뱃돈을   주셨어요.
Grandmother   us          New Year's money   gave.

오빠가       나한테     선물을     줬어요.
My brother   me          a gift      gave.

상우는       상자에     선물을     넣었어요.
Sangwoo      the box     the gift    put.

선생님이     학생들에게  숙제를     내줬어요.
The teacher  students    homework    assigned.

친구가       나한테     문자를     보냈어요.
My friend    me          a text      sent.
```

The element lineup matches English Pattern 4. The only move: the predicate shifts to the very end.

## Comparison: English Patterns ↔ Korean Predicate Slots

| English Pattern | English Structure | Korean Slots | Korean Structure | Key Difference |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Pattern 1 | S + V | One-place | S + Predicate | Predicate position only |
| Pattern 3 | S + V + O | Two-place A | S + O + Predicate | O and Predicate swap |
| Pattern 2 | S + V + C | Two-place B | S + Complement + Predicate | Predicate position only |
| Pattern 4 | S + V + IO + DO | Three-place | S + IO + DO + Predicate | Predicate position only |
| — | No equivalent | Two-place C | S + Required Adverbial + Predicate | Korean-only structure |

English patterns and Korean predicate slots are **mostly parallel in terms of elements needed**.
Three decisive differences:

1. **Word order** — English is SVO, Korean is SOV
2. **Required adverbials** — a mandatory element that exists only in Korean
3. **Particles** — what makes Korean's flexible word order possible
