# 사이시옷 — The Spelling Rule Sound Made

You already met compounds like 빗소리 (sound of rain) and 햇빛 (sunlight) in [Day 9](/worksheet/day9). Back then, the focus was on the pattern ("비+소리," "해+빛") — but read them out loud, and something's off.

비(rain) + 소리(sound) = 빗소리   [비쏘리]   (not the sound you'd get from just reading 비 and 소리 back to back)
해(sun) + 빛(light) = 햇빛       [핻삗]     (not the sound you'd get from just reading 해 and 빛 back to back)

This reference explains why it sounds that way, and how that gets reflected in spelling. It'll make the most sense once you've gone through Day 9's compound word patterns.

---

## 1. Sound Comes First — Tensing (된소리되기)

사이시옷 looks like a spelling rule, but it actually starts as a **pronunciation phenomenon**.

When two native Korean nouns combine into a compound, the first sound of the second word sometimes shifts to a tensed consonant (경음).

```
바다(sea) + 가(edge)    →   [바다까] [pa.da.k͈a]     (가 is pronounced 까)
코(nose) + 등(back)     →   [코뜽] [kʰo.t͈ɯŋ]         (등 is pronounced 뜽)
비(rain) + 소리(sound)  →   [비쏘리] [pi.s͈o.ɾi]      (소리 is pronounced 쏘리)
```

This isn't something Korean speakers decide consciously. The sound just naturally shifts at the exact point where the two words meet.

The shift isn't always tensing, either. It shows up in two other ways as well.

```
바다(sea) + 물(water)   →   [바단물] [pa.dan.mul]      (an ㄴ sound appears before 물)
나무(tree) + 잎(leaf)   →   [나문닙] [na.mun.nip̚]      (an ㄴ sound appears twice)
```

All three come from the same root cause — the sound changes in an unexpected way exactly where two words join.

---

## 2. How Do You Write the Sound? — Inserting ㅅ

To reflect this sound change in spelling, Korean inserts a **ㅅ** at the end of the first word. That's 사이시옷.

```
바다(sea) + 가(edge)     →   바닷가 (sandy shore, riverside)   (ㅅ is added to "다," which had no 받침)
비(rain) + 소리(sound)   →   빗소리 (sound of rain)
해(sun) + 빛(light)      →   햇빛 (sunlight)   [핻삗] [hɛ.p͈it̚]
내(stream) + 가(edge)    →   냇가 (streamside)   [내까] [nɛ.k͈a]
```

The spelling is always just one ㅅ, but the pronunciation splits three ways depending on context — [바다까/바닫까], [바단물], and so on. 사이시옷 only marks "the sound changed here" — it doesn't unify the pronunciation rule itself.

---

## 3. When Does It Apply? — Three Conditions

사이시옷 doesn't attach to just any compound. All three conditions have to be met at once.

```
1. It has to be a compound word
   (a single morpheme doesn't qualify)

2. At least one part has to be native Korean
   (Sino-Korean + Sino-Korean compounds don't get 사이시옷 as a rule — exceptions in the next section)

3. The first word has to end in a vowel
   (there needs to be room for the ㅅ to attach)
```

Condition 2 trips people up. "At least one native Korean part" means Sino-Korean + native Korean combinations count too.

```
존대(尊待, respect, Sino-Korean) + 말(speech, native Korean) → 존댓말 (polite speech)   [존댄말] [tɕon.dɛn.mal]
최대(最大, maximum, Sino-Korean) + 값(value, native Korean)  → 최댓값 (maximum value)
```

---

## 4. The Six Sino-Korean Exceptions

As a rule, Sino-Korean + Sino-Korean compounds don't take 사이시옷.

```
초(焦, focus) + 점(點, point) → 촛점(x)   초점(o)
개(個, piece) + 수(數, number) → 갯수(x)   개수(o)
```

개수 in particular is a word native speakers frequently misspell as 갯수 — because it's actually pronounced [개ː쑤], with tensing. But both 초점 and 개수 are Sino-Korean + Sino-Korean, so they get no ㅅ in writing. It's a classic case of pronunciation and spelling pulling in different directions.

That said, there are exactly six Sino-Korean + Sino-Korean compounds that get 사이시옷 anyway. They've simply been used too long, too widely, to change the spelling at this point.

```
곳간(庫間, storehouse)   셋방(貰房, rented room)   숫자(數字, number)
찻간(車間, train car)    툇간(退間, porch space)   횟수(回數, number of times)
```

Even these six only take 사이시옷 in exactly this two-syllable form. Add another word in front, and the rule reverts to normal.

```
셋방(貰房, rented room) — takes 사이시옷
전세(傳貰, lease deposit) + 방 → 전세방(o)  전셋방(x)
월세(月貰, monthly rent) + 방 → 월세방(o)  월셋방(x)
```

찻간 and 툇간 are barely used these days, so in practice, remembering just **곳간·셋방·숫자·횟수** is enough.

---

## 5. So How Should You Study This?

사이시옷 is a **spelling rule**, not a speaking rule. The pronunciation already comes out naturally — the only question is how to write it.

Honestly, the rule is genuinely complicated. You have to know it's a compound word, know whether tensing or ㄴ-insertion shows up when you say it, know whether the compound is native Korean or Sino-Korean, and on top of all that, know whether it falls under the general rule or one of the exceptions.

Native Korean speakers get confused too. 개수/갯수, 전셋방/전세방 — these are genuinely common mistakes, and the National Institute of Korean Language fields questions about 사이시옷 constantly. There are enough exceptions and fine-grained conditions that even native speakers can't always predict the right answer just from the rule.

So there's no need to try to conquer 사이시옷. This is enough:

- Notice it when you see it — "ah, that's a compound word, that's why there's a ㅅ"
- Look it up when you're unsure
- Remember it barely matters for speaking — your pronunciation is already natural

Look back at the words from Day 9, and they'll read a little differently now.

```
빗소리 (sound of rain)   →   비(rain) + 소리(sound), tensing
햇빛 (sunlight)          →   해(sun) + 빛(light), tensing
존댓말 (polite speech)   →   존대(respect, Sino-Korean) + 말(speech, native Korean), ㄴ-insertion
```

## Wrapping Up

사이시옷 starts with sound and ends with spelling. Two words meet, the pronunciation shifts in an unexpected way, and 사이시옷 is the mark left behind in writing.

You don't need to memorize all of it. Next time you run into an unexpected ㅅ in an unfamiliar word, come back and look it up here.
